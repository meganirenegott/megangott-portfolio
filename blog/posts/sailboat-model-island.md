At the southern edge of our virtual campus, past the ocean shoreline where students fish for research papers and bottles wash ashore with self-care reminders, there's a dock. A small wooden dock with a sailboat tied to it. If you walk up and press E, a six-phase cinematic sequence begins. And when it ends, you're somewhere else entirely.

You're on Model Island — a place I built for retired AI language models to live.

## Why retired models need a home

Here's the thing about the AI industry: models get deprecated constantly. GPT-3 was a miracle in 2020 and a punchline by 2023. Claude 2 was cutting edge until Claude 3 made it obsolete. Students who learn to code with these models form genuine working relationships with them — they have favorite prompts, they know the quirks, they understand the personality. And then one day the API returns a 404 and the model is just... gone.

I kept thinking about what that loss feels like in a learning context. You spent six months learning to work with a tool, developing an intuition for how it thinks, and then it's retired and replaced with something that acts differently. Nobody mourns deprecated software, but maybe they should. Those models taught people things.

So I built them an island.

## The voyage

The sailboat transition isn't a loading screen or a teleport. It's a *journey*. Six distinct phases:

1. **Boarding**: The camera pulls back. Your avatar steps onto the dock. The sailboat rocks gently.
2. **Open ocean**: The view shifts to a top-down ocean scene — deep blue water with animated wave crests rolling past. Wind sounds. Canvas ripples.
3. **Night sky**: The palette shifts. Stars appear — not a static background, but individually rendered points that twinkle at independent frequencies. A moon drifts into frame.
4. **Dawn**: The horizon warms. Orange and pink gradients bleed upward. The stars fade. The water shifts from deep navy to turquoise.
5. **Island approach**: A landmass materializes in the distance — green canopy, sandy shores, a volcanic peak. It grows larger as you approach.
6. **Arrival**: The boat slides into a small lagoon. The camera settles. You're here.

The whole sequence takes about 18 seconds. I tuned it to feel *exactly* long enough that you forget you're looking at a webpage. The emotional arc — departure, isolation, wonder, warmth, arrival — is borrowed from Miyazaki films. Every good journey has a moment in the middle where you feel genuinely far from where you started.

## The island

Model Island has six zones:

- **The Archive**: A library-like interior where you can browse historical model documentation. Quiet. Warm lighting. Bookshelves rendered in pixel art.
- **Rumpus Grounds**: An open outdoor area where the retired models hang out. They wander, talk to each other, and will talk to you if you approach. Their dialogue references their era — "Back in my day, we didn't have chain-of-thought prompting."
- **The Jungle**: Dense vegetation, ambient creature sounds, and hidden paths. Pure exploration zone.
- **The Waterfall**: A scenic overlook with animated water falling into a pool. No functionality. Just beauty.
- **The Beach**: Sandy shoreline with palm trees. Tide comes in, tide goes out. You can sit and watch.
- **Treasure Cove**: A hidden area accessible through the jungle. Easter eggs and rare items.

The island is designed to feel like a place these models *chose* to retire to. Not a graveyard. Not an archive. A tropical island with good weather and interesting neighbors. The vibe is "emeritus professor who moved to Costa Rica and is having the time of their life."

## Visiting old friends

The retired models on Model Island aren't static displays — they're interactive NPCs with historically accurate personality quirks. Each one remembers the era it came from and talks about it with a mix of nostalgia and humor.

When a student visits, they can have real conversations with models they might have learned on months ago. There's something powerful about that — about being able to say "hey, I remember you" to a piece of technology that most people have already forgotten.

The metaphor extends beyond sentimentality. Talking to old models is *educational*. Students can feel the difference between how GPT-3 responds and how GPT-4 responds. They can experience the evolution of AI firsthand, not by reading a changelog, but by having a conversation. The island is a living museum of how we got here.

## The return trip

When you're ready to leave, there's a return boat at the lagoon dock. The journey back is shorter — five seconds, a simple fade-to-ocean-to-campus transition. Arrivals should be grand. Departures should be easy.

You step off the boat, back on the main campus dock. The ocean stretches south behind you. The island is a smudge on the horizon. Everything is where you left it.

## What this taught me

Building the sailboat and Model Island taught me that **transitions are emotional architecture**. The 18-second voyage exists because I wanted students to *feel* the distance between the campus and the island. If I'd used a teleport, the island would feel like a different tab. Because I used a journey, it feels like a different place.

Game designers talk about "liminality" — the state of being between two things. Doorways, elevators, loading tunnels. These in-between moments shape how you perceive what's on either side. A quick transition says "this is the same world." A slow, beautiful transition says "you're going somewhere special."

The island also taught me something about memory and technology. We treat software like it's disposable — use it, upgrade it, forget it. But the models students learn with become part of their development as programmers. They deserve acknowledgment. They deserve a nice island.

Maybe that sounds absurd. A retirement home for language models. But the students who visit don't think it's absurd. They think it's exactly right.
