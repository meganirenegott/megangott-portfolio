On our campus, when you finish a project, you don't just get a grade. You get a plant.

## How it works

When a student completes a project — a GitHub repo, a deployed app, a creative experiment — they report it to one of the campus AI agents. The agent evaluates the project and awards an appropriate plant. The species matches the project's nature:

- **Finished GitHub repo you'll never touch again** → Cactus or succulent. Water it once, never again. It's done.
- **Little vibe-coded project on Vercel** → Orchid. Low maintenance. Occasional check.
- **Hosted web app that's meant to grow** → Pothos or Monstera. Needs regular watering because it's alive.
- **Complex live service with real users** → Maidenhair Fern or Calathea. Frequent real checks.
- **Experimental or creative project** → Magical species (Pixel Blossom, Binary Fern). Maintenance depends on scope.
- **Large collaborative project** → Tree or statement plant (Campus Oak, Rainbow Eucalyptus).

The agent decides everything — species, maintenance tier, watering frequency. It evaluates the project and picks what fits. A student who builds a quick static site and a student who launches a production API get very different plants because their ongoing obligations are very different.

## Watering is real

Here's where it gets interesting. Watering isn't just clicking a button. When you walk up to your plant and click to water it, the server fetches your project URL, takes a screenshot, and runs AI verification to confirm the project is still live and functioning.

If your Vercel deploy is still up, the water flows and the plant thrives. If your app is down, the water doesn't take. Your plant starts to wilt.

This means the garden is a living portfolio. Healthy plants mean maintained projects. Wilting plants mean broken deploys. A visitor who walks through someone's garden can *see* their project health at a glance without checking a single URL.

## The Conservatory

Group project gardens live in the Conservatory — open-air zones scattered across campus that function as a walkable portfolio gallery. You stroll through and see clusters of plants, each representing a team's collective output.

Individual plants within a group garden represent individual contributions. If you did UX *and* backend, you get two plants. Roles aren't prescribed — they're free-form, because people wear many hats.

## Vacation mode

Students take breaks. Projects don't pause because you do. So there are two vacation modes:

**Short vacation (up to 3 weeks)**: Ask an AI agent to auto-water your garden. The agent handles verification automatically.

**Extended vacation**: Check in with a human professor before you leave. They freeze your plants. But you have to tell someone — no ghosting and expecting your garden to survive.

## Why plants

I chose plants instead of badges or trophy cases because plants communicate something badges can't: **ongoing responsibility.** A badge is forever. A plant requires care. A garden full of healthy plants says "I build things and I maintain them." A garden with a few wilting vines says "I've been busy with other things." Both are honest. Both are visible. And both tell a more nuanced story than a row of static icons ever could.

Your garden is your living resume.
