The Alabaster Tower is our campus research library. Inside, each floor represents a year — 2023, 2024, 2025, 2026 — with the research, projects, and work produced by that cohort. It's a growing archive.

On the outside, it literally gets taller.

## Time as architecture

The tower's exterior on the campus map isn't a static sprite. It's a dynamically composed stack of column segments, one per year. Each segment has a distinct visual style that represents its era:

- **2023 (base)**: Solid marble foundation with classical columns. The origin. Heavy and permanent.
- **2024**: Crystal lattice segment, slightly translucent. The first expansion.
- **2025**: Neon-veined alabaster with glowing cyan lines. Technology bleeding into stone.
- **2026**: Ethereal light construct, barely solid, holographic. The newest layer, still forming.
- **Future years**: Procedurally varied from a palette of segment styles.

The building's rendered height on the map equals `base_height + (segment_height × active_floor_count)`. Each year the campus runs, the tower gets one segment taller. The clickable area and name label adjust to the composite height automatically.

## Why visible time matters

Most software is timeless in the worst way — it looks the same whether it's been running for a week or a decade. There's no patina. No growth rings. No visible history.

The Growing Tower rejects that. It's a building that *shows its age* — not as decay, but as accumulation. A student who joins in 2026 sees four segments and knows, visually, that three years of work came before them. A student who returns in 2028 sees six and knows the community continued after they left.

The tower is a monument to continuity. Every year adds a layer. The layers don't replace each other — they stack. The 2023 marble is still at the bottom, solid as the day it was placed. The newest layer is always at the top, still glowing, still forming.

It's a small thing. But walking past a building that's taller than last year says something about time and persistence that no changelog ever could.
